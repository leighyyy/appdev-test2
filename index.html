Understanding

Climate Change

Climate change is a pressing global issue caused mainly by the burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas. These release greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide and methane, trapping heat in the atmosphere and leading to rising temperatures, extreme weather, rising sea levels, and biodiversity loss.

The shift to renewable energy—such as solar, wind, hydro, and geothermal—is key to combating climate change. These clean energy sources reduce greenhouse gas emissions, provide sustainable power, and lessen our dependence on fossil fuels. By embracing renewable energy, we can protect the planet, secure a healthier future, and create a more sustainable world for generations to come.

Impacts of Climate Change

Hurricanes Will Become Stronger and More Intense

Scientists project that hurricane-associated storm intensity and rainfall rates will increase as the climate continues to warm.

More Droughts and Heat Waves

Droughts in the Southwest and heat waves (periods of abnormally hot weather lasting days to weeks) are projected to become more intense, and cold waves less intense and less frequent.

Longer Wildfire Season

Warming temperatures have extended and intensified wildfire season in the West, where long-term drought in the region has heightened the risk of fires. Scientists estimate that human-caused climate change has already doubled the area of forest burned in recent decades. By around 2050, the amount of land consumed by wildfires in Western states is projected to further increase by two to six times. Even in traditionally rainy regions like the Southeast, wildfires are projected to increase by about 30%.

Global Temperatures Will Continue to Rise

Summer of 2023 was Earth's hottest summer on record, 0.41 degrees Fahrenheit (F) (0.23 degrees Celsius (C)) warmer than any other summer in NASA’s record and 2.1 degrees F (1.2 C) warmer than the average summer between 1951 and 1980.

Arctic Is Very Likely to Become Ice-Free

Sea ice cover in the Arctic Ocean is expected to continue decreasing, and the Arctic Ocean will very likely become essentially ice-free in late summer if current projections hold. This change is expected to occur before mid-century.

Changes in Precipitation Patterns

Climate change is causing shifts in global precipitation patterns, leading to more extreme weather events. Some regions experience increased rainfall and flooding, while others suffer from prolonged droughts. These changes disrupt water availability, agriculture, and ecosystems, affecting food production and water supplies.

Renewable Energy

Renewable energy comes from natural sources like the sun, wind, water, and Earth's heat, which replenish over time. Unlike fossil fuels, these clean energy sources produce little to no greenhouse gas emissions, making them essential in the fight against climate change. By reducing carbon footprints and promoting sustainability, renewable energy plays a key role in creating a greener and more resilient future.

Hydropower

Harnesses flowing water to produce electricity

Solar Energy

Converts sunlight into electricity using solar panels

Wind Energy

Uses wind turbines to generate power

Geothermal Energy

Taps into Earth's internal heat for power and heating.

Primary energy consumption from renewable sources

About one-seventh of the world's primary energy comes from renewable technologies, including hydropower, solar, wind, geothermal, wave, tidal, and modern biofuels. This data, calculated using the 'substitution method,' accounts for inefficiencies in fossil fuel production. The share of renewables reflects total energy consumption across electricity, transport, and heating.

Types of Renewable Energy

Hydropower

Hydropower, or hydroelectric power, is a renewable energy source that generates electricity by utilizing the movement of water, typically through dams, rivers, or turbines. As one of the oldest and most reliable forms of renewable energy, hydropower provides a steady supply of electricity with minimal environmental impact compared to fossil fuels. It is widely used worldwide, contributing to energy security and reducing reliance on non-renewable resources.

How it fights climate change?

Unlike coal or natural gas power plants, hydropower does not rely on burning fossil fuels, significantly lowering carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions. This helps slow down global warming and reduces the harmful effects of climate change.

Hydropower plants can store water in reservoirs and release it as needed to generate electricity. This flexibility helps balance the energy grid, especially when integrating other renewable sources like solar and wind, which depend on weather conditions. By ensuring a stable energy supply, hydropower reduces the need for fossil fuel-based backup power.

Hydropower facilities often play a role in water conservation, flood control, and irrigation. By managing water resources effectively, hydropower helps maintain ecosystems, reduce the risk of natural disasters, and ensure water availability for communities. This contributes to long-term environmental sustainability and resilience against climate change.

Hydropower generation

Hydroelectric power has been a major low-carbon energy source for over a century and remains the largest renewable energy source, excluding traditional biomass. It accounts for about half of global renewable generation, though its contribution varies by country.

Solar Energy

Solar energy is a renewable energy source that harnesses sunlight to generate electricity or heat. It is captured using technologies like photovoltaic (PV) panels and solar thermal systems. Solar power is abundant, sustainable, and reduces reliance on fossil fuels, making it an eco-friendly energy alternative.

How it fights climate change?

Solar energy generates electricity without burning fossil fuels, significantly cutting carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other greenhouse gas emissions that contribute to global warming.

By replacing coal, oil, and natural gas with solar power, we reduce pollution and slow the depletion of non-renewable resources.

Traditional energy sources release pollutants that harm air quality, leading to health problems. Solar power helps create cleaner air, reducing respiratory diseases and environmental damage.

Wind Energy

Wind energy is a renewable energy source that harnesses the power of moving air to generate electricity. Wind turbines convert kinetic energy from the wind into mechanical power, which is then transformed into electrical energy using a generator. It is a clean, sustainable, and increasingly cost-effective alternative to fossil fuels.

How it fights climate change?

Wind energy generates electricity without burning fossil fuels, significantly cutting carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other greenhouse gas emissions that contribute to global warming.

By replacing coal, oil, and natural gas with wind power, we can reduce environmental damage caused by mining, drilling, and transportation of fossil fuels.

Unlike fossil fuel-based power plants, wind energy does not release pollutants like sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ), which contribute to smog, acid rain, and respiratory diseases.

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy is a renewable energy source that harnesses heat from the Earth's interior to generate electricity or provide direct heating. This heat originates from the Earth's core and is accessed through underground reservoirs of steam and hot water. Power plants and heating systems use this energy by drilling wells and utilizing turbines or heat exchangers to convert the heat into usable energy.

How it fights climate change?

Unlike fossil fuels, geothermal energy produces minimal carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other greenhouse gases, significantly lowering its environmental impact.

Geothermal power is available 24/7, unlike solar and wind energy, which depend on weather conditions. This reliability helps reduce dependence on fossil fuels.

Geothermal heat pumps efficiently regulate building temperatures, reducing the need for conventional energy sources and lowering overall carbon emissions.
